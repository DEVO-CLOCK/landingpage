Hey. So you want to get into tech but you don't know where to start. Maybe you've tried before and gave up because there was just too much information pulling you in different directions. Maybe someone told you to "just learn Python" or "just do a bootcamp" and it still didn't click.

This kit is not going to overwhelm you. We're going to go section by section, and by the time you finish reading, you'll have a clear picture of what to learn first, what to ignore for now, and how to actually make progress without burning out in week two.

One rule before we start: stop trying to learn everything at once. That's the number one reason people quit. Pick a lane, go deep, build something, then expand. That's the whole secret.

01
First things first
What kind of builder do you want to be?

Before you install anything or watch a single tutorial, you need to answer this question. Because the path for someone who wants to build mobile apps is completely different from someone who wants to build websites. And both are different from someone who wants to automate things or work with data.

Here are the main lanes. Read through them and pick the one that genuinely excites you, not the one that sounds most impressive:

A
Frontend Web Developer
You build the things people see and click on. Websites, web apps, landing pages. You care about how things look and feel. If you like design and creativity alongside code, this is probably your lane. Start here: HTML, CSS, JavaScript.
B
Backend Developer
You build the engine behind the scenes. Databases, servers, logic that users never see but always feel. If you like problem solving and systems, this is your lane. Start here: Python or Node.js.
C
Mobile App Developer
You build apps for phones. If your first thought when you see a problem is "there should be an app for this," this is you. Start here: React Native (works for both Android and iPhone) or Flutter.
D
No-Code and Automation
You build workflows, bots, and systems using tools instead of writing a lot of code. This is actually a serious career path and extremely useful for Nigerian SMEs. Start here: n8n, Zapier, Bubble, or Glide.
Honest advice: if you genuinely have no idea, start with Frontend. It gives you visible results fast, which keeps you motivated. You can always branch out later. Most people who say they don't know what they want just need to build something and see how it feels.
02
The honest part
How long is this actually going to take?

People either tell you it takes three months or they tell you it takes ten years. Both are technically true depending on what you mean by "learning to code." Let's be more specific.

1
2 to 4 weeks to understand the basics
You'll know how things work. You can read code. You can follow along with tutorials without feeling completely lost. This is the "okay I get it now" stage.
2
2 to 3 months to build your first real thing
A simple project you actually made yourself, not just copied from a tutorial. Something you can show people and say "I built this." This is the goal of your first semester.
3
4 to 6 months to be genuinely useful to a client
You can take on a small paid job. Build someone a portfolio site, a simple tool, a landing page. This is when the ₦ starts.
The truth nobody tells you
"The people who learn fastest are not the smartest ones. They're the ones who build something every single week, no matter how small. Watching tutorials alone will not make you a developer. Building things will."
03
Your first 90 days
A simple roadmap that actually works

This is for the Frontend lane because that's the best starting point for most people. If you chose a different lane, the structure is the same but the tools change. Ask us and we'll map it out for you.

Weeks 1 to 2
Learn HTML and CSS
Build the structure and style of a webpage. By the end of week two you should be able to build a basic personal webpage from scratch. Not pretty yet, but working.
Weeks 3 to 5
Learn JavaScript fundamentals
This is where things start to feel like real programming. Variables, functions, logic. It will feel hard for a few days. Push through. Everyone hits this wall and everyone gets past it.
Weeks 6 to 8
Build your first project
No more tutorials. Close them. Build something from scratch. A to-do list, a calculator, a simple quiz app. It doesn't have to be impressive. It has to be yours.
Weeks 9 to 10
Learn Git and put your work online
Git is how developers save and share their code. GitHub is where it lives. Set up your profile and push your project there. Now you have a portfolio.
Weeks 11 to 12
Build one more project, bigger this time
Take what you've learned and build something with a real use case. A landing page for a small business, a simple booking form, anything that solves a real problem. This becomes your first portfolio piece.
End of Month 3
You're ready for your first client conversation
You won't be perfect. You'll still Google things constantly. That's fine. Every professional developer does the same thing. You now know enough to be useful and to keep learning on the job.
04
Free tools only
Where to actually learn this stuff

You don't need to pay for anything in your first six months. Everything below is free and genuinely good. The problem is not access to resources, it's choosing one and sticking with it long enough to finish.

✦
The Odin Project
The best free full curriculum for web development. It gives you a structured path, explains the why not just the how, and makes you build real projects throughout. Start here if you want frontend or full stack.
theodinproject.com Free
✦
freeCodeCamp
Structured courses with certifications at the end. Good for people who like clear milestones. Their JavaScript Algorithms and Data Structures course is solid.
freecodecamp.org Free
✦
CS50 by Harvard
If you want to actually understand how computers work underneath, this is the one. It's a full university course taught by one of the best lecturers in the world and it costs nothing. Hard but worth it.
cs50.harvard.edu Free
✦
YouTube: Traversy Media and Kevin Powell
Brad Traversy for general web dev, Kevin Powell specifically for CSS. Both are clear, practical, and don't waste your time. Use these to supplement, not replace, a structured curriculum.
YouTube Free
A word of warning: tutorial hell is real. It's when you watch tutorial after tutorial and feel like you're learning but you're actually just watching. The fix is simple. For every one hour of tutorial you watch, spend two hours trying to build something yourself. Even if it breaks. Especially if it breaks.
05
The money part
When and how to get your first paid gig

Yes, you can make money before you're "good." And no, you don't need to be on Upwork or Fiverr right away. Your first clients are closer than you think.

1
Start with people you already know
A family member's small business that has no website. A classmate selling things on Instagram who needs a landing page. A lecturer who needs a simple form built. These are your first clients. Charge small, do a great job, get a testimonial.
2
Charge something, even if it's small
Don't work for free unless it's for a charity or a specific portfolio reason. Even ₦10,000 for a landing page tells the client this is professional work. Free work rarely gets taken seriously and rarely gets finished.
3
Under-promise and over-deliver
Quote longer timelines than you think you need. Build faster than you promised. Your client will think you're exceptional. This is how referrals happen.
4
Join Dev O'Clock membership for referrals
Once you're a member we route client leads to you directly. We help you pitch, handle the awkward pricing conversations, and support you through your first delivery. You're not doing it alone.
06
The mindset bit
The things that actually separate people who make it from those who don't

This section might sound soft but it's probably the most important one. The technical stuff is learnable by anyone. The stuff below is what actually determines whether you stick with it.

✓
Build something every week
It doesn't have to be big. A button that does something. A page you styled. A function you wrote. Consistent small output beats occasional bursts every time.
✓
Learn in public
Post what you're building on Twitter, WhatsApp, wherever. Even if it's ugly and unfinished. It creates accountability, attracts people who want to help, and builds your reputation before you even realise it.
✓
Get around people who are also building
This is genuinely the biggest cheat code. Your environment shapes your consistency more than any resource or tutorial. One session at Dev O'Clock will show you what we mean.
✓
Be okay with not understanding everything immediately
You will Google things. You will ask for help. You will copy a solution and not fully understand it yet. That is normal. That is literally how every developer works. The goal is to understand it eventually, not instantly.
